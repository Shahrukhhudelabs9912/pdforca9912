## JPG vs. PNG: Which Format Should You Choose?

Most PDF-to-image tools offer both JPG and PNG output. Here's when to use each:

| Factor | JPG | PNG |
|--------|-----|-----|
| **File size** | Smaller (lossy compression) | Larger (lossless compression) |
| **Photo quality** | Excellent for photos, gradients, complex images | Identical to source — no quality loss |
| **Text sharpness** | Slight softening at lower quality settings | Perfectly crisp text edges |
| **Transparency** | No transparency support | Supports transparent backgrounds |
| **Best for** | Social media, presentations, general sharing | Screenshots, diagrams, text-heavy documents, design work |

**For most PDF conversions, JPG is the right choice.** It produces smaller files with excellent visual quality. Choose PNG only when you need pixel-perfect text, transparency, or lossless archival quality.

## Maintaining Quality: Tips for the Sharpest Images

1. **Start with a high-quality PDF.** A blurry scan converted to JPG will produce a blurry image. The conversion preserves quality — it doesn't enhance it.

2. **Match DPI to your use case.** Don't use 120 DPI for a social media story — 72 DPI is sufficient and produces a smaller file.

3. **Convert text-heavy PDFs at higher quality.** Small text can appear slightly soft at 72 DPI. If readability matters, step up to 96 or 120 DPI.

4. **For presentations, use 96–120 DPI.** Slides projected on a large screen benefit from higher resolution, especially for data-dense charts and small labels.

5. **Check the output before sharing.** Open the converted JPG at 100% zoom. If text is readable and images are sharp, the quality setting is correct. If it looks soft, re-convert at a higher DPI.

## FAQ

**Does converting to JPG reduce the quality?**
JPG uses lossy compression, so there's a minimal quality reduction compared to the original PDF vector content. At High (96 DPI) and above, the difference is invisible for all practical purposes. For zero quality loss, choose PNG output.

**Can I convert a password-protected PDF?**
You'll need to unlock it first using [Unlock PDF](/unlock-pdf), then convert the unlocked version to JPG.

**What's the maximum file size I can upload?**
The tool handles PDFs up to 100 MB. For very large files, consider [compressing the PDF](/compress-pdf) first to speed up upload and conversion.

**Will the JPG include clickable links from the PDF?**
No. Images are static — hyperlinks, form fields, and interactive elements don't transfer. The image captures the visual appearance only.

**Can I convert just one page from a multi-page PDF?**
Yes. Select the specific page number during conversion. You don't need to convert the entire document if you only need one page.

**Does this work with scanned PDFs?**
Yes. Scanned PDFs are already image-based internally, so the conversion captures exactly what you see. For better results with scanned documents, consider running [OCR](/ocr-pdf) first if you need searchable text, or convert directly to JPG if you just need the visual.

**How is this different from taking a screenshot?**
Screenshots capture your screen at screen resolution and may include browser UI, scroll bars, or incomplete pages. PDF to JPG converts at a resolution you choose (up to 300 DPI), captures the full page regardless of screen size, and produces a clean image without any interface artifacts.
