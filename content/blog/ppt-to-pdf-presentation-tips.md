## Why Convert PowerPoint to PDF?

A `.pptx` file is an editable, software-dependent format. A PDF is a portable, final one. Converting makes sense whenever you want your slides to be:

- **Universal** — PDFs open in any browser, on any device, with no PowerPoint license required.
- **Consistent** — Fonts and layout are baked in, so nothing shifts on the recipient's screen.
- **Locked** — Recipients can view but not accidentally (or intentionally) edit your content.
- **Acceptable to portals** — Colleges, job boards, and government sites often accept only PDF.
- **Smaller and easier to send** — A PDF is usually lighter than a bloated PPTX full of embedded media.

## Why Fonts and Layout Sometimes Break

Before you convert, it helps to know what causes the classic "it looked fine on my screen" problem:

- **Font substitution** — Your deck uses a font the other system doesn't have. It gets swapped for a lookalike, and text reflows — pushing bullets, boxes, and titles out of place.
- **Linked (not embedded) media** — Images or videos linked from your hard drive won't travel with the file.
- **Custom themes and templates** — Corporate templates with precise positioning can drift if the conversion engine interprets spacing differently.
- **Slide transitions and animations** — These are motion effects. A PDF is static, so animations flatten to their final state (more on this below).

Converting straight to PDF with a reliable tool sidesteps most of these — because the fonts and positioning are locked into the output.

## What Happens to Animations and Transitions?

This is the most common surprise, so it's worth being clear: **a PDF is a static document.** It cannot play animations, transitions, embedded videos, or GIFs.

When you convert, each slide becomes one page showing its **final state** — every element that would appear after all animations have run is visible at once. This is usually exactly what you want for a handout or a document to share. If your slide *builds* content step by step (e.g., bullets appearing one at a time), consider whether the flattened version still reads clearly. If not, you can duplicate the slide in PowerPoint to show each build stage before converting.

## Tips for a Perfect Conversion

**Use standard fonts.** Arial, Calibri, Times New Roman, and other common fonts convert perfectly every time. Decorative or downloaded fonts are the most likely to be substituted.

**Embed fonts before converting** (if using PowerPoint's export): File → Options → Save → "Embed fonts in the file." This travels the exact fonts with your deck.

**Embed media, don't link it.** Insert images directly rather than linking to a file path, so they're part of the presentation.

**Check your slide size.** Standard 16:9 (widescreen) converts cleanly. If you designed in 4:3 or a custom size, preview the PDF to confirm nothing is cropped.

**Flatten complex builds.** If a slide relies heavily on animation to make sense, duplicate it into static stages before converting.

## FAQ

**Does it work with both .ppt and .pptx files?**
Yes. The tool accepts both the older `.ppt` format and the modern `.pptx`. For the most reliable results, `.pptx` is preferred — if your file is `.ppt`, open it in PowerPoint and save as `.pptx` first if you can.

**Will my animations and transitions be in the PDF?**
No — PDFs are static. Each slide converts to a single page showing its final state. Animations, transitions, and embedded videos don't play in a PDF.

**Can I convert on my phone?**
Yes. The [PowerPoint to PDF tool](/powerpoint-to-pdf) works in any mobile browser. Upload your `.pptx` from Files (iPhone) or your file manager (Android), convert, and download — no app needed.

**My fonts changed after converting — why?**
The conversion system didn't have your exact font and substituted the closest match. Use standard fonts, or embed fonts in PowerPoint (File → Options → Save → Embed fonts) before converting.

**Can I convert a Google Slides presentation?**
Google Slides can export to PDF directly (File → Download → PDF Document). Alternatively, download it as `.pptx` and use this tool.

**Will the PDF include my speaker notes?**
By default, no — the PDF contains only the slides as your audience sees them. If you need a notes version, export "Notes Pages" from PowerPoint separately.
