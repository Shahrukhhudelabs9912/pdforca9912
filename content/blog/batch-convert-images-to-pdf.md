You've got a folder with 137 images — scanned receipts, photos of handwritten notes, product screenshots, or pages of a document someone photographed one by one. You need them as a single, ordered PDF, and converting them one at a time would take the rest of your afternoon.

This is a surprisingly common job. Field workers photograph paperwork on their phones, students snap textbook pages, accountants collect receipts, and designers gather reference shots. The result is always the same: a pile of loose images that needs to become one tidy document.

The manual approach — open each image, print to PDF, merge, repeat — is slow and error-prone. Batch conversion does the whole set at once, in order, in a couple of minutes. This guide shows you how, and how to get a clean, correctly ordered result the first time.

## What You'll Need

- Your images (JPG, PNG, or other common formats)
- A web browser on your phone or laptop
- No signup, no app, no payment

## Why Batch Conversion Beats One-at-a-Time

Converting images to PDF one by one means uploading, converting, downloading, and repeating for every single file. With 100 images, that's 100 repetitions — and then you still have to merge them all into one document.

Batch conversion handles the whole set in a single upload. You select all your images at once, set the order, and get one PDF back. The difference in time is not marginal; it's the difference between a two-minute job and an afternoon.

| One-at-a-time approach | Batch conversion |
|------------------------|-----------------|
| Upload, convert, download per image | Upload all at once |
| 100 separate PDFs to merge afterward | One PDF, ready to download |
| Easy to lose track of order | Set order before converting |
| Slow on any connection | Done in minutes |

The other advantage is consistency. Every page in a batch-converted PDF uses the same settings — same page size, same orientation, same margins. When you convert one by one, small differences creep in.

## Before You Start: Sort and Name Your Images

The order of pages in your PDF depends on the order you upload the images. A little preparation here saves you from reordering pages afterward.

- **Name files sequentially.** `001.jpg`, `002.jpg`, `003.jpg` sorts correctly in any file browser. Avoid names like `IMG_4521.jpg` mixed with `scan_final_v2.jpg` — the sort order will be unpredictable.
- **Delete duplicates and blanks.** Scroll through the folder quickly and remove any accidental duplicates or completely blank shots.
- **Rotate before uploading.** If some photos are sideways, rotate them in your phone's gallery or a photo editor first. You can also use the [Rotate PDF tool](/rotate-pdf) on the finished PDF if you miss any.
- **Check the format.** JPG and PNG work universally. If you have HEIC files from an iPhone, convert them to JPG first (your phone's share menu usually offers this).

Open the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf) in your browser. Select all your images at once — hold Ctrl (or Cmd on Mac) and click to select multiple files, or drag the whole folder's contents onto the upload area. You can upload dozens of images in one go.

After uploading, the tool shows you a preview of each image as a page. Drag and drop to reorder them if needed. This is the moment to catch anything out of sequence — much easier to fix here than after the PDF is built.

### Step 3: Convert and Download

Click convert. The tool processes every image and assembles them into a single PDF, in the order you set. Download the finished file when it's ready.

### Step 4: Check the Result

Open the PDF and scroll through it. Confirm the pages are in the right order, images are sharp, and nothing is missing. For a large batch, spot-check the first page, last page, and a few in the middle.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Name files sequentially before uploading.** This is the single biggest time-saver for getting the order right.
- **Rotate images first.** Fix sideways photos before converting, or use the [Rotate PDF tool](/rotate-pdf) afterward.
- **Match image quality to purpose.** High resolution for printing or portfolios; lower is fine for quick sharing.
- **Convert the whole batch at once.** Don't split it into small groups — one upload keeps the order and settings consistent.
- **Compress after, not before.** Build the full PDF first, then compress the finished file if you need it smaller.
- **Keep originals.** Don't delete your source images until you've confirmed the PDF is correct.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Uploading in random order.** If files aren't named sequentially, the pages come out jumbled. Sort first.
- **Forgetting sideways photos.** A landscape shot in a portrait document is jarring. Rotate before or after.
- **Mixing formats without checking.** Convert HEIC, TIFF, and WebP to JPG before batch-uploading.
- **Skipping the preview check.** Always confirm the order before hitting convert on a large batch.
- **Ignoring file size.** A 100-image PDF can be huge. Compress it if you plan to email or upload it.
