## What You'll Need

- Your finished assignment (Word, Google Docs, or similar)
- A web browser on your phone or laptop

## Why Submit as PDF at All?

Professors ask for PDFs for a reason. A Word file looks different on every machine, depending on which fonts are installed, which version of Office is running, and the page settings on that computer. Open the same `.docx` on your laptop and your professor's, and your carefully placed table might jump to the next page.

A PDF freezes the layout. What you see is exactly what your professor sees, on any device, forever. Nothing shifts, no font gets swapped, and the file can't be accidentally edited during grading.

| Word document (.docx) | PDF |
|-----------------------|-----|
| Layout shifts between devices and Office versions | Looks identical everywhere |
| Fonts swap if not installed on the reader's machine | Fonts stay locked in |
| Easy to edit — accidental or otherwise | Fixed, read-only by default |
| Larger, sometimes won't open on phones | Universal — opens on any phone or browser |

That's why "submit as PDF" is the near-universal rule for assignments, dissertations, and reports.

## Before You Convert: Format the Document Right

The cleanest PDF starts with a well-formatted source document. Fix these in Word or Google Docs first — they're much harder to change after conversion.

- **Set the page size to A4.** Most Indian and international universities expect A4, not US Letter. Check File > Page Setup.
- **Use standard margins.** 1 inch (2.54 cm) all around is the safe default unless your rubric says otherwise.
- **Pick a common font.** Times New Roman, Calibri, or Arial at 12 pt for body text. Exotic fonts are the number-one cause of "it looked different when I opened it."
- **Add a cover page if required.** Name, roll number, course code, submission date, and your professor's name — check your department's format.
- **Number your pages.** Insert page numbers in the footer so a printed copy stays in order.
- **Run a spell check.** Fixing typos after conversion means re-exporting the whole file.

Many departments require a specific file name, like `RollNo_Name_CourseCode.pdf`. Rename the file to match exactly before uploading. A wrong file name has cost students marks on strict portals.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Convert from the final version.** Make every edit in Word first. Fixing a typo after conversion means re-exporting the whole file.
- **Embed your fonts.** A proper Word-to-PDF conversion locks fonts in, so your professor sees exactly what you designed. This is why exporting to PDF beats emailing a `.docx`.
- **Keep images reasonable.** A 12-megapixel photo dropped into a report bloats the file. Resize large images before converting, or compress the PDF afterward.
- **Match the rubric exactly.** Page size, margins, font, line spacing, cover page details — strict graders deduct marks for format, not just content.
- **Keep a backup.** Save both the Word source and the final PDF. If the portal rejects the file, you can re-export in seconds.
- **Submit early.** Portals slow to a crawl near deadlines. A PDF ready an hour ahead saves you from a frozen upload screen.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Uploading the Word file by mistake.** Double-check the file extension is `.pdf` before you hit submit.
- **Ignoring the size limit until it's too late.** Check the file size the moment you convert, not at 11:58 PM.
- **Using a decorative font.** If your professor's computer doesn't have it and fonts weren't embedded, your assignment looks broken. Stick to standard fonts.
- **Submitting multiple separate files when one was requested.** Merge them into a single PDF.
- **Skipping the final read-through.** Always open the PDF and scroll through it before uploading.
