You have a 300-page PDF — a contract, a manual, a scanned report, a bank statement running the whole year — and you only need pages 45 to 52. Maybe it's the one section a colleague asked for, or the two forms buried in a giant document, or a single chapter you want to study without scrolling past everything else.

Sending the whole file is wasteful and sometimes risky: it might contain pages you'd rather not share. Printing just the pages you need and re-scanning them is slow and lowers quality. What you actually want is to lift those exact pages out into their own clean PDF.

That's page extraction, and it takes about a minute. This guide walks through how to do it, when to use extraction versus splitting, and how to get exactly the pages you need without touching the rest.

## What You'll Need

- The PDF you want to extract pages from
- A web browser on your phone or laptop
- The page numbers you need (a range, or a scattered few)
- No signup, no app, no payment

## Extract vs. Split: Which Do You Need?

These two actions sound similar, and people mix them up. The difference is simple once you see it.

- **Extracting** pulls out the specific pages you choose and gives you one new PDF containing just those pages. The original stays whole. This is what you want when you need "pages 45 to 52" or "pages 3, 8, and 12."
- **Splitting** breaks a PDF into multiple separate files — for example, turning a 100-page PDF into 100 single-page files, or cutting it into chunks at set intervals.

If your goal is "give me just these pages as one document," extraction is the right tool. If it's "chop this into several files," splitting is.

## Understanding Page Selection

Before you extract, be clear on exactly which pages you want. There are two common patterns:

- **A continuous range**, like pages 45 to 52. This is the most common case — one chapter, one section, one form set.
- **Scattered pages**, like pages 3, 8, and 12. Useful when the pages you need aren't next to each other, such as pulling only the signature pages from a long contract.

Open the PDF first and note the exact page numbers. Be careful: the number printed on the page (from the document's own numbering) may not match the PDF's page count, especially if there's a cover, a table of contents, or roman-numeral front matter. Go by the PDF viewer's page counter, not the printed page number.

## What Happens to the Original?

A common worry: does extracting pages damage or change the source PDF? It doesn't. Extraction creates a new file from the pages you selected and leaves the original exactly as it was. You can extract different pages from the same document as many times as you like without affecting it.

That said, always keep your original until you've confirmed the extracted file is correct — good practice with any document operation.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Go by the PDF viewer's page count.** The printed page number can differ from the PDF's actual page position.
- **Open the file first.** Confirm the exact pages before extracting so you don't have to redo it.
- **Extract, don't re-scan.** Pulling pages keeps the original quality; printing and re-scanning degrades it.
- **Combine steps.** Extract, then merge or organize to build precisely the document you want.
- **Compress if the extract is still large.** A few pages of heavy scans can still be big; the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) helps.
- **Keep the original.** Don't delete the source file until you've verified the extract.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Confusing printed and actual page numbers.** Front matter throws off the count. Use the viewer's counter.
- **Extracting the wrong range.** Double-check start and end pages before you commit.
- **Sending an extra page by accident.** Always open the result, especially for sensitive documents.
- **Using split when you meant extract.** Splitting makes multiple files; extraction gives you one. Pick the right tool.
