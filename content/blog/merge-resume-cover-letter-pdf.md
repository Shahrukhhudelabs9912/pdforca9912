The job posting says "upload one document," but you have two: a resume and a cover letter, each its own file. Some application portals only accept a single upload. Others technically allow two, but sending one clean PDF looks more organized and makes sure the recruiter reads them in the right order.

You could paste one into the other and hope the formatting survives, but that usually ends in mangled spacing and mismatched fonts. There's a cleaner way.

Merging your resume and cover letter into a single PDF keeps each document exactly as you designed it, puts them in the order you want, and gives the recruiter one tidy file to open. This guide walks through how to do it, which order to use, and how to keep the result looking sharp.

## What You'll Need

- Your resume as a PDF
- Your cover letter as a PDF
- A web browser on your phone or laptop
- No signup, no app, no payment

## Why Merge Instead of Sending Two Files

Sending a single PDF isn't just about following upload rules. It's about controlling the experience for the person reading your application.

**Upload limits.** Many application systems accept only one attachment. If you have two documents, you either merge them or leave one out. Merging solves this cleanly.

**Reading order.** When you send two separate files, you can't control which one the recruiter opens first. A merged PDF guarantees your cover letter is read before your resume, or whichever order you choose.

**Professional impression.** One well-organized document looks more polished than two loose files with different naming. It signals attention to detail, which matters when you're being judged on exactly that.

**Formatting stays intact.** Unlike copying text between documents, merging keeps each page exactly as you designed it. Fonts, spacing, and layout are preserved because you're combining finished PDFs, not re-flowing text.

| Sending two files | Sending one merged PDF |
|---|---|
| May exceed a one-upload limit | Fits a single-upload requirement |
| Recruiter picks the order | You control the order |
| Two files to name and track | One tidy file |
| Risk of one being overlooked | Everything read together |

## Which Order: Cover Letter or Resume First?

The standard convention is cover letter first, then resume. The cover letter is your introduction — it frames who you are and why you're applying, so it makes sense to lead with it. The resume then backs up that pitch with detail.

Think of it like a conversation: the cover letter says "here's why I'm a fit," and the resume says "here's the proof." Leading with the letter sets context before the reader dives into dates and job titles.

There are exceptions. If a specific job posting asks for the resume first, follow the posting. When in doubt, cover letter first is the safe, expected choice.

## Step-by-Step: Merge Your Resume and Cover Letter

### Step 1: Make Sure Both Are PDFs

Before merging, both documents need to be PDFs. If your cover letter or resume is still a Word document, convert it first with the [Word to PDF tool](/word-to-pdf). Converting to PDF also locks your formatting, so a recruiter opening it on any device sees exactly what you designed.

Working from PDFs is what keeps fonts and spacing intact through the merge. A PDF is a finished snapshot, so combining two of them never re-flows your text.

### Step 2: Open the Merge Tool and Upload Both Files

1. Open the [Merge PDF tool](/merge-pdf)
2. Upload your cover letter and your resume — drag and drop both, or click to browse
3. Both files appear as items you can arrange

### Step 3: Set the Order

Drag the files into the order you want. For the standard layout, put the cover letter first and the resume second. Double-check the order before merging — this is the sequence the recruiter will read.

If your cover letter is one page and your resume is two, the merged file will be three pages in that exact order.

### Step 4: Merge and Download

1. Click merge to combine the files into one PDF
2. Download the result
3. Open it and page through to confirm the order and formatting are correct

### Step 5: Name It Professionally

Give the merged file a clear, professional name before uploading it. Something like `Jane-Doe-Application-Marketing-Manager.pdf` tells the recruiter exactly what it is and who it's from.

Avoid names like `final_v3_REAL.pdf` or `document.pdf`. A clean filename is a small detail that reinforces the organized impression the merged PDF already gives.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Convert to PDF before merging** — it locks your formatting so nothing shifts
- **Cover letter first** unless the posting says otherwise
- **Check the page order** after merging, every time
- **Use a professional filename** with your name and the role
- **Keep it concise** — a merged file shouldn't balloon into ten pages; stick to what's asked
- **Compress if the file is large** — some portals cap upload size; the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) shrinks it without visible quality loss
- **Preview on a different device** if you can, to confirm it looks right everywhere

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Merging in the wrong order** — resume before cover letter reads backward to most recruiters
- **Copying text between documents instead of merging PDFs** — this wrecks formatting
- **Forgetting to convert Word files first** — merge tools work on PDFs, so convert with the [Word to PDF tool](/word-to-pdf)
- **Using a sloppy filename** — it's the first thing the recruiter sees
- **Ignoring upload size limits** — compress the file if a portal rejects it
- **Not reviewing the final PDF** — always page through before submitting
- **Padding the package** — only include what the posting asks for
