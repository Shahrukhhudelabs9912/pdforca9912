## Two Kinds of PDF Forms

Before you fill anything in, it helps to know which type of form you're dealing with, because they behave very differently.

| Interactive form | Flat form |
|------------------|-----------|
| Has clickable fields you can type into | Just a printed-looking page, no fields |
| Cursor turns into a text caret over blanks | Clicking does nothing |
| Built with form fields by the creator | Scanned, or exported without fields |
| Type directly and save | Needs text added on top of the page |

An **interactive form** was built with real form fields. Click a blank and a cursor appears — you type, tab to the next field, and you're done. Most modern government and corporate forms are like this.

A **flat form** has no fields at all. It might be a scanned paper form, or a document someone exported without keeping the fields. Clicking does nothing because there's nothing to click. These are the ones that send people hunting for Acrobat — but they're just as easy to handle once you know the approach.

Quick test: open the PDF and hover over a blank line. If your cursor becomes a text caret or the field highlights, it's interactive. If nothing happens, it's flat.

## Step-by-Step: Filling an Interactive Form

### Step 1: Open It in Your Browser

Most browsers — Chrome, Edge, Firefox, Safari — have a built-in PDF viewer that recognizes interactive form fields. Open the PDF and you'll usually see the fields highlighted or ready to accept text.

Click the first field and start typing. Use Tab to jump to the next field, and click checkboxes or dropdowns as needed.

### Step 2: Fill Every Field Carefully

Work through the form top to bottom. Double-check names, dates, and numbers as you go — it's easy to tab past a field and leave it blank. For date fields, match the format the form asks for.

### Step 3: Save the Filled Copy

Use your browser's save or download option to keep a copy with your entries baked in. Save it under a clear name so you don't confuse it with the blank original.

### Step 4: Add a Signature if Required

If the form needs a signature, open the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf), upload your filled form, and add your signature where it belongs. Download the signed version, and your form is complete.

## Step-by-Step: Filling a Flat Form

This is the case that usually sends people looking for Acrobat. A flat form has no clickable fields — but the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf) doesn't just add signatures. It lets you place text anywhere on the page, which is exactly what a flat form needs.

### Step 1: Open the Sign PDF Tool

Head to the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf) and upload your flat form. You'll see the full page ready to work on.

### Step 2: Place Text on the Blanks

Add a text box over each blank line or box on the form and type your answer. Position it so it sits neatly on the line, and match the size to the surrounding print. Repeat for every field — name, address, date, and so on.

### Step 3: Tick Boxes and Mark Choices

For checkboxes or yes/no options, add a small "X" or a tick mark in the right spot. For multiple-choice sections, place your mark against the correct option.

### Step 4: Add Your Signature

Sign in the signature area using the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf) — draw it, type it, or upload an image of your signature. Position it on the signature line and size it to look natural.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Check the form type first** — hover over a blank; a text caret means interactive, no response means flat
- **Match the surrounding text size** — text that's too big or small looks out of place and can overflow the line
- **Don't cover printed labels** — position your text on the blank, not over the field's name
- **Save under a new name** — keep the blank original separate from your filled copy
- **Review before sending** — open the finished PDF once to confirm nothing is misaligned or missing
- **Compress only if needed** — if a portal enforces a size limit, use the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) after filling, not before

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Starting an Acrobat trial you don't need** — a flat form fills just fine in the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf)
- **Printing, signing, and rescanning** — it wastes paper and produces a messy, oversized file
- **Typing over the wrong line** — zoom in on flat forms so your text lands exactly where it should
- **Forgetting a field** — flat forms don't tab between blanks, so scan the whole page before downloading
- **Sending an unsigned form** — double-check the signature line if the form requires one
