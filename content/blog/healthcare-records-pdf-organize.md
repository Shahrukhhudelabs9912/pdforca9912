You're sitting in the waiting room, and the doctor's office has just asked for your previous test results. You start scrolling through your phone: a lab report in your email, a prescription photo in your camera roll, a scan of an old X-ray report buried in a messaging app, a discharge summary somewhere in your downloads. By the time you find half of them, your name is being called.

This scramble is familiar to anyone managing their own health, or a parent's or child's. Medical documents pile up from different labs, hospitals, and clinics, each in its own format, scattered across apps and folders. When a doctor needs the full picture, handing over ten separate files is slow and easy to get wrong.

The fix is simple: one clean, well-ordered PDF that holds your relevant records in a sensible sequence. A doctor can open it once and read your history top to bottom. This guide shows you how to build that file in a few minutes, without installing anything or creating an account.

## What You'll Need

- Your medical documents — PDFs, scans, or phone photos
- A web browser on your phone or computer
- No signup, no app, no payment

Everything below runs in your browser.

## The Typical Medical Document Set

Before you combine anything, it helps to know what you're gathering. Depending on your situation, your records might include some mix of these:

| Document | Where it comes from | Why the doctor wants it |
|----------|--------------------|--------------------------|
| Lab and blood test reports | Diagnostic lab | Current and trending health markers |
| Imaging reports (X-ray, MRI, CT, ultrasound) | Radiology center | Structural findings |
| Prescriptions | Previous doctors | What you're currently taking |
| Discharge summary | Hospital | Details of a past admission |
| Vaccination or immunization record | Clinic | History for children and travel |
| Allergy and medication list | You or your GP | Safety before prescribing |
| Referral letters | Referring doctor | Reason for the visit |
| Insurance or ID documents | You | Registration and claims |

You rarely need every category for a single visit. Bring what's relevant to the reason you're going, plus a short summary of your ongoing conditions and medicines.

## Step-by-Step: Combine Medical Records Into One PDF

### Step 1: Convert Photos and Scans to PDF First

Many of your records will be phone photos or scanned images — a paper prescription, a printed lab slip. Combining tools work best with PDFs, so convert images first with the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf).

Photo tips for medical documents:

- Shoot in bright, even light near a window
- Lay the page flat and hold the phone directly overhead
- Make sure every value, unit, and date is sharp and readable
- Capture the whole page, including the lab letterhead and reference ranges

Save each with a clear name like `blood-test-2026-08.pdf` or `cardiology-prescription.pdf`.

### Step 2: Gather Your Existing PDFs

Lab portals, hospitals, and imaging centers usually email or provide reports as PDFs already. Download them into one folder so everything sits in one place before you combine.

## Getting the Order Right

A random order makes a busy doctor work harder. A logical order lets them follow your history the way they think about it. A sensible sequence:

```
Page 1:  One-page summary (conditions, current medicines, allergies)
Page 2:  Referral letter (if you have one)
Page 3:  Most recent lab reports
Page 4:  Older lab reports (for comparison over time)
Page 5:  Imaging reports (X-ray, MRI, CT)
Page 6:  Current prescriptions
Page 7:  Discharge summary from any past admission
Page 8:  Vaccination record (for children or travel)
```

The principle: **most relevant and most recent first.** Lead with a short summary so the doctor sees the big picture in ten seconds, then let them drill into the details. Grouping lab reports by type and ordering them newest to oldest makes trends easy to spot.

A one-page summary at the front is the single most useful thing you can add. List your ongoing conditions, current medicines with doses, known allergies, and the reason for this visit. It turns a pile of reports into a briefing.

## Meeting Upload and Size Limits

If you're uploading to a hospital patient portal or emailing your records, size limits apply.

| Destination | Typical size limit |
|-------------|--------------------|
| Hospital patient portal | 5–10 MB per file |
| Email attachment (Gmail, Outlook) | 25 MB total |
| WhatsApp document share | 100 MB (slow above 10 MB) |
| Telehealth app upload | 5–10 MB |

A merged file full of high-resolution scans can hit 15–25 MB. If yours is too big, open the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf), start with medium compression, download, and confirm every value and date is still readable before sending.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Bringing everything instead of what's relevant.** A 50-page dump of every test you've ever had slows the doctor down. Bring what's relevant to this visit.
- **No summary page.** A list of conditions, medicines, and allergies at the front saves the doctor from hunting through the whole file.
- **Wrong date order.** Newest results first, so trends are obvious without flipping back and forth.
- **Blurry photos of paper reports.** An unreadable lab value is worse than no value. Reshoot in better light.
- **Forgetting to compress.** A large file may be silently rejected by a patient portal. Compress before uploading.
- **Uploading to untrusted tools.** Medical records contain sensitive personal and health data. Use a privacy-respecting tool that doesn't store your files.

## Privacy: Medical Records Are Sensitive

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How should I order my medical records in the PDF?

Put a one-page summary first — conditions, current medicines, allergies, and the reason for the visit. Then most recent lab reports, older labs for comparison, imaging reports, current prescriptions, and any discharge summaries. Most recent and most relevant first is the guiding principle.

### Is it safe to merge medical records online?

Only if you use a tool that processes your file in the browser or deletes it right after, with no account required. Medical records contain highly personal data. Check the privacy policy before uploading, and avoid any service that stores or shares your files.

### My lab report PDF is password-protected. How do I merge it?

You need to remove the password first. Open the file with the correct password (labs often use your date of birth or a reference number), then use the [Unlock PDF tool](/unlock-pdf) to save an unlocked copy. Merge that copy. Only do this for records that are yours.

### What if my merged file is too large to upload to the hospital portal?

Use the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf). Start with medium compression, which usually brings a large file down to a fraction of its size while keeping text and numbers readable. Download and verify every value is still legible before uploading.

### Can I do this on my phone?

Yes. Photograph paper documents in good light, convert them to PDF with the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf), then merge everything with the [Merge PDF tool](/merge-pdf). Both tools work in a mobile browser. You can share the result directly from your phone at the appointment.
