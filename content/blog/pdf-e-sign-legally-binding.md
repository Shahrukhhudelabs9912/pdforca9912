## What Is an Electronic Signature?

An electronic signature is any electronic indication of intent to agree to or approve the contents of a document. This is a deliberately broad definition — it covers a typed name at the bottom of an email, a drawn signature on a touchscreen, a scanned image of a handwritten signature placed on a PDF, and cryptographically secured digital signatures.

The key legal concept is **intent**. An e-signature is legally binding when it demonstrates that the signer intended to sign, that the signer can be identified, and that the document has not been altered after signing.

It helps to understand the difference between two terms that are often confused:

| Term | What it means | Legal weight |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Electronic signature | Any electronic mark indicating intent to sign | Legally valid in most jurisdictions for most documents |
| Digital signature | A cryptographically secured e-signature using a certificate | Higher assurance; required for some regulated documents |

A drawn signature on a PDF using a browser tool is an electronic signature. A signature created with a government-issued digital certificate (like India's DSC or the EU's qualified electronic signature) is a digital signature. Both can be legally binding — the difference is the level of assurance and the use cases each is suited for.

## The Legal Framework by Jurisdiction

### United States

The ESIGN Act (2000) and UETA (Uniform Electronic Transactions Act) establish that electronic signatures are legally valid for most contracts and documents in the US. An e-signature has the same legal effect as a handwritten signature as long as both parties consented to use electronic signatures and the signature demonstrates intent.

Most everyday contracts — employment agreements, NDAs, service contracts, lease agreements — are fully enforceable with an e-signature.

### European Union

The eIDAS Regulation (2016) governs electronic signatures across EU member states. It defines three tiers:

- **Simple Electronic Signature (SES):** A basic e-signature, such as a typed name or drawn signature. Valid for most everyday documents.
- **Advanced Electronic Signature (AES):** Linked to the signer, capable of identifying them, and detects changes after signing. Higher assurance.
- **Qualified Electronic Signature (QES):** Created with a qualified certificate on a secure device. Legally equivalent to a handwritten signature across all EU member states.

For most business contracts, a simple or advanced e-signature is sufficient. QES is required for specific regulated transactions.

### United Kingdom

Post-Brexit, the UK retained its own Electronic Communications Act 2000 and the eIDAS-derived framework. E-signatures are valid for most contracts. The Law Commission confirmed in 2019 that electronic signatures can be used to execute most documents, including deeds, subject to witnessing requirements.

### India

The Information Technology Act 2000 and its amendments recognize electronic signatures. Aadhaar-based e-signatures (using OTP authentication) are legally valid for most documents. Digital Signature Certificates (DSC) issued by licensed Certifying Authorities are required for specific government filings, company registrations, and income tax submissions.

### Australia and Canada

Both countries have legislation recognizing electronic signatures for most commercial contracts. Australia's Electronic Transactions Act 1999 and Canada's PIPEDA framework both establish that e-signatures are valid where the parties consent to electronic transactions.

## Documents Where E-Signatures May Not Be Valid

Even in jurisdictions with strong e-signature laws, certain document types are commonly excluded or require additional formalities:

| Document type | Typical requirement |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Wills and testaments | Wet ink signature, witnesses; some jurisdictions now allow e-wills |
| Real estate deeds | Notarization; some jurisdictions accept e-notarization |
| Court documents | Jurisdiction-specific; many courts now accept e-filing |
| Powers of attorney | Often requires notarization or witnessing |
| Government identity documents | Always require physical signatures |
| Negotiable instruments (cheques) | Wet ink in most jurisdictions |

When in doubt about a specific document type, check with a legal professional or the relevant authority in your jurisdiction.

## What Makes an E-Signature Hold Up

Signing a PDF is easy. Making sure that signature would hold up if challenged is a separate matter. Courts and counterparties generally look for four things:

1. **Intent to sign.** The signer clearly meant to sign, not just view the document. A drawn or typed signature placed deliberately on a signature line demonstrates this.
2. **Consent to electronic signing.** Both parties agreed to conduct the transaction electronically. This can be explicit (a clause in the contract) or implied by conduct (they sent you a document to e-sign).
3. **Attribution.** The signature can be linked to the signer. Email trails, timestamps, and identity verification strengthen attribution.
4. **Integrity.** The document has not been altered after signing. This is where digital signatures with tamper-evidence add value over simple drawn signatures.

For a coffee-shop freelance agreement, a drawn signature on a PDF is fine. For a high-value contract or a regulated filing, you may want the stronger assurance that a certificate-based digital signature or a dedicated e-signature platform with audit trails provides.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Confirm both parties consent** to electronic signing — a short line in the document or an email exchange establishes this clearly
- **Keep the email trail** — timestamps and sender identity strengthen the evidence that you signed
- **Check the document type** — wills, deeds, and some notarized documents may still need wet ink or witnessing
- **Use a certificate-based digital signature** for high-value or regulated documents where tamper-evidence matters
- **Store the signed copy safely** — a signed contract you can't find later is a practical problem even if it was legally valid
- **Sign the final version** — make sure you're signing the agreed-upon final draft, not an earlier version
- **Add the date** — many signature lines expect a date alongside the signature

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Assuming all documents accept e-signatures** — a few document types still require physical signatures or notarization
- **Not keeping evidence** — a signed PDF with no email trail or timestamp is weaker if challenged
- **Signing an unread document** — the signature binds you to the full contents; read before you sign
- **Confusing an image paste with a secure signature** — for low-stakes documents this is fine, but high-value contracts benefit from stronger methods
- **Ignoring jurisdiction differences** — what's valid in one country may have extra requirements in another

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a drawn signature on a PDF legally binding?

In most jurisdictions, yes. A drawn signature that demonstrates your intent to sign, where both parties consented to electronic signing, is legally binding for most everyday documents. You can create one with the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf). For high-value contracts or regulated filings, a certificate-based digital signature offers stronger assurance. This is general information, not legal advice — check your local rules for sensitive documents.

### What's the difference between an electronic signature and a digital signature?

An electronic signature is any electronic mark showing intent to sign — a typed name, a drawn signature, or a signature image. A digital signature is a specific type of electronic signature secured with a cryptographic certificate that verifies the signer's identity and detects any changes to the document. Both can be legally binding; digital signatures provide higher assurance and are required for certain regulated documents.

### Can I use an e-signature for a contract in another country?

Usually, yes, but the rules vary. Most major economies — the US, EU, UK, India, Australia, Canada — recognize electronic signatures for common commercial contracts. However, the required level of signature (simple, advanced, or qualified) and the excluded document types differ by jurisdiction. For cross-border contracts, confirm the requirements in the relevant jurisdictions or consult a legal professional.

### Do both parties need to sign electronically?

Not necessarily. One party can sign electronically while the other signs with a pen, as long as both consent to the method used. It's common for one party to sign a PDF with the [Sign PDF tool](/sign-pdf) and send it on for the other party to sign the same file. What matters is that both signatures reflect genuine intent to agree.

### What documents can't be signed electronically?

This varies by jurisdiction, but commonly excluded documents include wills, real estate deeds, powers of attorney, court documents, and certain government identity documents. Many of these require notarization, witnessing, or wet-ink signatures. Some jurisdictions are gradually allowing electronic versions with additional safeguards. Always check the specific requirements for legally sensitive documents.
