## Why a PDF, and Not Just a Photo

Most students already have a photo of their certificate. So why do portals insist on PDF? A few practical reasons:

| Photo (JPG/PNG) | PDF |
|-----------------|-----|
| Size varies wildly, often 4-8MB | Can be compressed to fit strict limits |
| One file per page — messy for multi-page docs | Multiple pages in one clean file |
| Looks casual, can be edited easily | Looks official, harder to tamper with |
| Some portals reject image uploads outright | Accepted almost everywhere |

Government and education portals — think college ERP systems, the National Scholarship Portal, state admission sites — are built around PDF uploads. A PDF keeps every page together, holds a fixed layout, and fits neatly inside the 1MB, 2MB, or 5MB caps these sites love to enforce.

If your certificate has front and back content, or comes with a mark sheet, a PDF lets you combine everything into one file instead of uploading three separate images and hoping the portal accepts them.

## Step-by-Step: Convert Your Certificate to PDF

### Step 1: Take a Clean Photo First

The quality of your PDF depends entirely on the photo you start with. Before you convert anything, get a good capture:

1. Place the certificate flat on a plain, dark table — a wooden desk or a dark cloth works well
2. Use daylight from a window, not the overhead tube light that creates glare
3. Hold your phone directly above, parallel to the paper, so the edges look straight
4. Fill the frame with the certificate, leaving just a small border
5. Check that every line of text is sharp and readable before you move on

Take the photo in your phone's normal camera. Avoid heavy filters or "beauty" modes that can smudge fine print like your registration number or the school seal.

### Step 2: Open the JPG to PDF Tool

Once you have a clean photo, open the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf) in your browser. It works the same on Android, iPhone, or a laptop.

Upload your photo — tap to browse your gallery, or drag the file in on a laptop. If your certificate is more than one page, upload all the images together so they land in a single PDF.

### Step 3: Arrange the Pages in Order

If you uploaded more than one image, put them in the right sequence. A common set-up for admissions is: School Leaving Certificate first, then the mark sheet, then any supporting page. Drag the thumbnails to reorder them so the reviewer sees your documents in a logical flow.

Double-check the orientation too. A certificate photographed sideways should be rotated upright before you finish — nobody wants an admissions officer tilting their head to read your details.

### Step 4: Download Your PDF

Generate the PDF and download it. You now have a single file with every page in order, ready to rename something clear like `Rahul_Sharma_SLC.pdf`. A tidy filename helps you find it fast when the portal asks for it, and looks more professional than `IMG_20261005_193344.pdf`.

### Step 5: Check the File Size

Open the file and confirm it looks correct — all pages present, text readable, nothing cut off. Then check the size. If your portal caps uploads at 2MB or 1MB and your PDF is bigger, don't re-shoot the photo. Run it through the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) to shrink it while keeping the text legible.

Most single-page certificate PDFs compress comfortably under 1MB without visible quality loss, which clears almost every government and college limit.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Shoot in daylight, not under a tube light** — window light avoids glare on the glossy certificate paper and the school seal
- **Keep the paper flat** — press out folds with a book for a minute before shooting, so text doesn't curve
- **Fill the frame** — get close enough that the certificate fills the photo, leaving only a thin border
- **Convert before compressing** — make the PDF first, then shrink it only if the size limit demands it
- **Name the file clearly** — `Name_SLC.pdf` beats a random camera filename when a portal asks for it
- **Save a master copy** — keep one good PDF on your phone and in your email so you never redo this
- **Never crop out the seal or signature** — portals verify these; a cropped certificate can get your application rejected

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Uploading the raw JPG** — many portals silently reject image formats; always convert to PDF first
- **Ignoring the size limit** — a 6MB scan will bounce off a 2MB portal; use the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) instead of guessing
- **Photographing at an angle** — skewed, keystoned images look unprofessional and can make text hard to verify
- **Splitting a multi-page document** — keep the certificate and its mark sheet in one PDF when the form asks for a single file
- **Heavy filters or scanner-app "enhance"** — over-processing can turn fine print into mush; a clean, honest photo reads better
- **Waiting until the deadline night** — prepare the PDF early so a portal glitch doesn't cost you the seat
