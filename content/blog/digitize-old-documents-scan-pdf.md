Somewhere in your home or office there is a stack of paper that represents years of your life — old tax returns, property deeds, medical records, school certificates, insurance policies. You know roughly what is in there, but finding a specific document means going through the whole pile. And if something gets lost, damaged, or destroyed, it is gone.

Digitizing those documents solves both problems at once. A scanned PDF is searchable, shareable, and backed up. You can find any document in seconds by typing a keyword. You can send a copy to your accountant, your lawyer, or a family member without touching a printer. And if the original paper is ever lost in a flood, a fire, or a move, you still have every word.

This guide covers the full process — from scanning with your phone or a flatbed scanner, to running OCR so the text is actually searchable, to organizing and storing your digital archive. No special equipment required. You can start with just your phone.

## What You'll Need

- A smartphone (any modern Android or iPhone) or a flatbed scanner
- A web browser for OCR processing
- The documents you want to digitize
- No app purchase, no signup, no payment required for the core workflow

## Understanding the Two Steps: Scan and OCR

Most people think of digitizing as just scanning — taking a photo or scan of the paper. That gets you an image of the document, but not a searchable one. A scanned PDF without OCR is essentially a photograph: you can see the text, but you cannot search it, copy it, or have a screen reader read it aloud.

The second step — OCR, or Optical Character Recognition — reads the image and converts the visual text into actual machine-readable characters. After OCR, you can press Ctrl+F (or Cmd+F) in any PDF viewer and search for any word in the document. That is the difference between a digital filing cabinet and a digital photograph album.

| Type | What it is | Searchable? | File size |
|------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Scanned image PDF | Photo of the page | No | Large |
| OCR PDF | Text layer added over the scan | Yes | Similar or smaller |
| Native digital PDF | Created by software (Word, etc.) | Yes | Small |

The goal of this guide is to get you from paper to OCR PDF — the most useful format for long-term document storage.

## Step-by-Step: Digitize Paper Documents Into Searchable PDFs

### Step 1: Scan the Document

You have two main options depending on what you have available.

**Option A: Phone scanning (most common)**

Modern smartphones produce scans good enough for almost any document. Use the built-in scanner in your phone's notes or files app, or a dedicated scanning app. The key is to use the document scanning mode — not just the camera — because it automatically corrects perspective, crops to the page edges, and enhances contrast.

- iOS: Notes app → tap the camera icon → Scan Documents
- Android: Google Drive app → tap the + button → Scan
- Both platforms: Microsoft Lens (free) is an excellent dedicated option

Tips for a clean phone scan:
- Place the document on a dark, non-reflective surface
- Shoot in good natural light — avoid direct sunlight, which creates glare
- Hold the phone directly overhead, parallel to the document
- Make sure all four corners are visible before capturing
- For multi-page documents, scan all pages in one session so they save as a single file

**Option B: Flatbed scanner**

A flatbed scanner produces higher quality results, especially for old or fragile documents, photographs, and anything with fine print. For most home and office use, 300 DPI is the right resolution — it captures all readable detail without creating an unnecessarily large file. Use 600 DPI only for photographs or documents with very small text.

Save the output as PDF, not as individual image files. Most scanner software has a "Scan to PDF" option.

### Step 2: Get a PDF File

If you scanned with a phone app, export or save as PDF. Most scanning apps have a share or export button that lets you save directly as PDF to your files or cloud storage.

If your scanner saved individual image files (JPG or PNG), convert them to PDF first using the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf). Upload all the page images, arrange them in order, and download as a single PDF.

### Step 3: Run OCR to Make the PDF Searchable

This is the step most people skip — and it is the most important one.

1. Open the [OCR PDF tool](/ocr-pdf)
2. Upload your scanned PDF
3. Select the document language (English, or whichever language your document is in)
4. Process and download the OCR PDF

The output looks identical to the input — same pages, same layout — but now contains a hidden text layer. Open it in any PDF viewer and press Ctrl+F (or Cmd+F on Mac) to search for any word. You can also select and copy text from the page.

### Step 4: Verify the OCR Output

OCR is not perfect, especially on old documents with faded ink, unusual fonts, or handwriting. Before filing the document away, do a quick check:

- Search for a word you know is on the page — a name, a date, a key term
- Try selecting a sentence and copying it — paste it somewhere to confirm the text is correct
- For financial or legal documents, verify that numbers and proper nouns were recognized correctly

If OCR missed a section, it is usually because that section was too faded, blurry, or handwritten. The image is still there and readable — OCR just could not extract the text from it.

### Step 5: Compress and Organize

Scanned PDFs can be large. Before storing or sharing, compress the file using the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf). A 10-page scan at 300 DPI typically compresses from 8–15 MB down to 1–3 MB with no visible quality loss.

Then name and organize the file clearly:
- Use a consistent naming format: `YYYY-MM-DD_document-type_description.pdf`
- Example: `2019-03-15_property-deed_flat-302-mumbai.pdf`
- Store in a folder structure that mirrors how you think about the documents: by year, by category, or by person

## Real-World Scenarios

### Digitizing a Decade of Tax Returns

You have a folder of paper tax returns going back ten years. Each year is a few pages of forms and receipts. You want them searchable and backed up before you recycle the paper.

1. Scan each year's documents as a separate PDF using your phone's document scanner
2. Name each file clearly: `tax-return-2015.pdf`, `tax-return-2016.pdf`, and so on
3. Run each through the [OCR PDF tool](/ocr-pdf)
4. Store in a folder called `Tax Returns` in your cloud storage

Now you can search across any year for a specific figure, employer name, or deduction type.

### Preserving Old Family Documents

You have your grandparents' property deed, a marriage certificate from the 1970s, and some old school certificates. The paper is yellowing and fragile.

1. Use a flatbed scanner at 300 DPI — gentler on fragile paper than a phone held overhead
2. Scan each document as a separate PDF
3. Run OCR — older documents may have lower OCR accuracy, but the image is preserved regardless
4. Back up to at least two locations: a cloud service and an external hard drive

Even if OCR cannot read faded handwriting perfectly, the scanned image is a permanent digital record of the original.

### Building a Medical Records Archive

You have years of paper medical records — prescriptions, lab reports, discharge summaries, vaccination records. Finding a specific test result means going through a thick folder.

1. Scan all records, grouping by type: lab reports, prescriptions, hospital records
2. Run OCR on each PDF
3. Name files with date and type: `2022-08-lab-report-thyroid.pdf`
4. Store in a folder structure: `Medical Records > 2022 > Lab Reports`

After OCR, you can search for any test name, medication, or doctor's name across your entire medical history in seconds.

### Digitizing a Business's Paper Archive

A small business has years of paper invoices, contracts, and correspondence. Moving to a new office is the trigger to finally go paperless.

1. Use a document feeder scanner for speed — most office scanners have one
2. Scan in batches by year and document type
3. Run OCR on each batch
4. Use the [Merge PDF tool](/merge-pdf) to combine related documents where useful (e.g., all invoices from one client into one file)
5. Store in a structured folder system with consistent naming

A fully OCR'd archive means any employee can find any document by searching for a client name, invoice number, or date.

## Tips for the Best Result

- Scan at 300 DPI for standard documents. Use 600 DPI only for photographs or documents with very small text — higher DPI means larger files without meaningful benefit for normal reading.
- Clean the scanner glass before scanning. Dust and smudges appear as marks on every page.
- For phone scanning, use the document mode (not the camera) — it corrects perspective and enhances contrast automatically.
- Scan in good light but avoid direct sunlight, which creates glare and washes out text.
- For multi-page documents, scan all pages in one session to get a single PDF rather than separate files per page.
- After OCR, always do a quick search test before filing the document away.
- Compress scanned PDFs before storing — a 10-page scan can drop from 15 MB to 2 MB with no visible quality loss using the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf).
- Back up to at least two locations. A single cloud folder is not a backup — it is one copy. Use cloud storage plus an external drive, or two different cloud services.
- For handwritten documents, OCR accuracy will be lower. The image is still preserved and readable; OCR just may not extract all the text correctly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need special software to make a PDF searchable?

No. The [OCR PDF tool](/ocr-pdf) runs in your browser — no software to install, no account to create. Upload your scanned PDF, select the language, and download the searchable version. The whole process takes under a minute for most documents.

### How accurate is OCR on old or faded documents?

OCR accuracy depends on scan quality. A clean, high-contrast scan of a typed document will be recognized almost perfectly. Faded ink, unusual fonts, or handwriting will produce lower accuracy — some words may be missed or misread. The image is always preserved regardless of OCR accuracy, so the document remains readable even where text extraction was imperfect. For critical documents, verify the OCR output by searching for a few key terms.

### What resolution should I use when scanning?

300 DPI is the standard for documents you want to read and archive. It captures all readable detail without creating unnecessarily large files. Use 600 DPI only for photographs or documents with very small text. For phone scanning, use the document mode in your scanning app — it handles resolution automatically and produces results equivalent to a 300 DPI flatbed scan for most documents.

### How should I store and back up my digitized documents?

Store in at least two locations. A single cloud folder (Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud) is one copy, not a backup. Use cloud storage plus an external hard drive, or two different cloud services. Organize with a consistent folder structure and naming convention so you can find files without searching. For very important documents — property deeds, birth certificates, passports — consider a third copy stored offsite or with a trusted family member.

### Can I digitize handwritten documents?

Yes, and the scan will be a permanent digital record of the original. OCR accuracy on handwriting is lower than on typed text — modern OCR handles printed handwriting reasonably well but struggles with cursive or unusual scripts. For handwritten documents, the primary value of digitizing is preservation and easy sharing, not full-text search. The image is always there and readable even where OCR could not extract the text.
