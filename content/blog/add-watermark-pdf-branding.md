A proposal goes out to a client without your company name on it — six months later, you find it shared on a competitor's desk with their branding on top. A draft document circulates internally, someone treats it as final, and the wrong version reaches a customer. A photographer's portfolio PDF gets downloaded and the images reappear on someone else's website.

Watermarks prevent all three scenarios. A visible stamp on every page — your logo, "CONFIDENTIAL," "DRAFT," or a copyright notice — marks ownership, signals document status, and discourages unauthorized redistribution.

This guide shows how to add watermarks to any PDF in seconds, for free, without Adobe Acrobat or any desktop software.

## Why Watermark a PDF?

Watermarks serve different purposes depending on context:

### Branding and Ownership

- **Proposals and reports** — Your company logo on every page reinforces brand presence
- **Portfolios** — A photographer or designer's name across images discourages theft
- **Course materials** — Training companies mark PDFs to prevent unauthorized sharing
- **Templates** — Mark free versions with your brand; remove for paid/premium versions

### Document Status

- **DRAFT** — Signals that the document isn't finalized; prevents premature circulation
- **CONFIDENTIAL** — Alerts readers that the content shouldn't be shared
- **FOR INTERNAL USE ONLY** — Restricts the intended audience
- **SAMPLE** or **PREVIEW** — Marks a document as non-final for client review
- **APPROVED** / **FINAL** — Confirms the document has been reviewed and accepted

### Legal Protection

- **Copyright notices** — "© 2026 Your Company. All Rights Reserved."
- **License restrictions** — "Licensed to [Client Name] only"
- **Do Not Copy** — Discourages photocopying and redistribution
- **Proof / Not for Production** — Marks pre-approval versions in publishing and printing

| Type | Best for | Example |
|------|----------|---------|

Text watermarks are faster to create. Image watermarks (your logo as a PNG with a transparent background) look more professional and are harder to remove.

Photographers, illustrators, and designers share portfolio PDFs with potential clients. Without protection, those high-resolution images can be extracted and used without permission:

1. Add your name or logo as a semi-transparent watermark across every page

## Choosing the Right Opacity

Opacity is the single most important setting. Too strong and the watermark obscures content. Too subtle and it's invisible (defeating the purpose).

| Opacity | Visibility | Best for |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| 10–20% | Barely visible, very subtle | Branding on client-facing final documents |
| 30–40% | Clearly visible but doesn't block content | Portfolio protection, licensing marks |
| 50–60% | Prominent, cannot be missed | DRAFT, CONFIDENTIAL, status labels |
| 70–80% | Dominates the page | SAMPLE, preview copies that shouldn't be used as-is |

**Start at 30%.** Open the result, read a page of text through the watermark, and adjust. You want the watermark to be immediately noticeable without making the document hard to read.

## Diagonal vs. Horizontal vs. Tiled

| Placement | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Diagonal (45°)** | Covers the most area; hardest to crop out | Can look aggressive on formal documents |
| **Horizontal (centre)** | Clean, professional look | Easy to crop if positioned poorly |
| **Corner** | Least intrusive | Easy to remove by cropping the page |
| **Tiled (repeated)** | Maximum coverage; impossible to remove | Can make the document harder to read |

For security-focused watermarks (anti-theft, confidentiality), use **diagonal or tiled**. For branding on professional documents, use **horizontal centre** or a **corner logo** at low opacity.

## Watermarks That Are Hard to Remove

A determined person with Acrobat Pro or a PDF editor can remove simple watermarks. To make removal harder:

1. **Use diagonal placement** — it intersects with text, images, and margins, making clean removal nearly impossible without damaging content
2. **Tile the watermark** — repeating it across the page means removing one instance still leaves dozens
3. **Use image watermarks** — text watermarks can sometimes be found and deleted as a PDF layer; rasterized image watermarks merge with the page content
4. **Lower the opacity but increase coverage** — a subtle watermark spread everywhere is harder to notice and remove than a bold one in one spot
5. **Combine with password protection** — use [Protect PDF](/protect-pdf) to restrict editing permissions after watermarking

No watermark is 100% removal-proof, but these techniques make it impractical for casual theft.

## Removing Watermarks Later

If you watermarked a draft and now need the clean final version:

- **If you kept the original:** Simply use the un-watermarked original file for the final version
- **If you only have the watermarked version:** You'll need to recreate the document or use an advanced PDF editor

**Best practice:** Always keep the clean, unwatermarked original saved separately. Add watermarks to copies, not to your only version.

## FAQ

**Does watermarking increase the file size?**
Minimally. A text watermark adds almost nothing. An image watermark (logo) adds the image's size — typically 50–200 KB spread across all pages. If the file becomes too large, [compress it](/compress-pdf) afterward.

**Can I watermark a password-protected PDF?**
First unlock it using [Unlock PDF](/unlock-pdf), then add the watermark, then re-protect with [Protect PDF](/protect-pdf) if needed.

**Will the watermark appear when the PDF is printed?**
Yes. The watermark is part of the page content — it appears on screen and in print identically.

**Can I add different watermarks to different pages?**
The tool applies one watermark to all pages. For different watermarks on different sections, split the PDF, watermark each part separately, then merge them back together.

**Is a watermarked PDF legally stronger than an unwatermarked one?**
A watermark isn't a legal mechanism by itself — but it establishes visual proof of ownership and intent. In copyright disputes, a consistently watermarked body of work supports your ownership claim.

**Can someone just screenshot the content to bypass the watermark?**
They can, but screenshots produce lower quality than the original PDF. For text-heavy documents, this isn't a practical way to steal content. For images, the quality loss makes screenshots unusable for commercial purposes.
