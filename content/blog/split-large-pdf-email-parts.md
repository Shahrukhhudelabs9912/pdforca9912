You attach the file, write the message, hit send, and a minute later it bounces back: "Message exceeds size limit." Your PDF is 38MB and your email service caps attachments at 25MB. Now what?

This happens constantly with scanned contracts, photo-heavy reports, catalogs, and multi-chapter documents. Email providers set hard limits on attachment size — Gmail and Outlook cap around 25MB, and many corporate servers are stricter still. A single big PDF simply won't go through.

There are two ways to fix it. You can shrink the file so the whole thing fits, or you can split it into smaller parts and send them across a couple of emails. This guide focuses on splitting, which is the right choice when the document is genuinely large or when the recipient only needs certain sections. It also covers when compressing is the smarter move instead.

## What You'll Need

- The large PDF you're trying to email
- A web browser on any device
- Knowledge of your email provider's attachment limit
- No signup, no app, no payment

## Know Your Email Attachment Limits

Before splitting, it helps to know the ceiling you're working under. These are the common caps:

| Email service | Attachment limit |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Gmail | 25MB (larger files auto-upload to Google Drive) |
| Outlook / Hotmail | 20–25MB |
| Yahoo Mail | 25MB |
| Corporate / Exchange servers | Often 10MB, sometimes less |
| iCloud Mail | 20MB (Mail Drop for larger) |

A useful rule: aim for each part to land comfortably under the limit, not right at it. Email encoding adds roughly 33% overhead to attachments, so a "24MB" file can actually arrive as more than 30MB on the wire. Target parts around 15–18MB to be safe on a 25MB limit.

## Split or Compress? Choosing the Right Fix

Splitting isn't always the answer. Sometimes shrinking the whole file is cleaner. Here's how to decide:

| Situation | Best approach |
|-----------|--------------|
| Recipient needs only certain sections | Split and send just those pages |
| Document is huge because of many scanned images | Compress first, split only if still too big |
| You want the recipient to have one file | Compress |
| The file must stay one document (a signed contract) | Compress, don't split |
| It's a long report with clear chapters | Split by chapter |

Often the best move is both: run the file through the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) first to knock down the size, and if it's still over the limit, split the compressed version into parts. Compressing first means fewer parts to send.

## Step-by-Step: Split Your PDF for Email

### Step 1: Open the Tool and Upload

1. Open the [Split PDF tool](/split-pdf)
2. Upload your large PDF — drag and drop, or click to browse
3. The tool loads the document and shows your page count

Everything runs in your browser, so a confidential contract or a report full of client data never leaves your device for someone else's server. For anything sensitive going out by email, that privacy is worth having.

### Step 2: Choose How to Split

Decide how to divide the document. Common approaches:

- **By page range** — split pages 1–20 into one file, 21–40 into another
- **By natural sections** — one part per chapter, report, or appendix
- **Into equal parts** — divide a 60-page file into three 20-page files

If your goal is purely to fit email limits, split roughly evenly by page count. If the recipient only needs specific sections, split along those boundaries so you can send just what they need.

### Step 3: Split and Download the Parts

Run the split and download each part. You'll get separate PDF files, each a slice of the original. Name them clearly so the recipient understands the order, for example `Report_Part1_of_3.pdf`.

### Step 4: Check Each Part's Size

Before attaching, confirm each file is under your email limit with room to spare. If any part is still too big, either split it again into smaller ranges or run that part through the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf) to bring it down.

### Step 5: Send with Clear Labels

Attach each part to your email and tell the recipient what they're getting. A short note like "Sending in 3 parts due to size — Part 1 covers pages 1–20" saves confusion. If parts are large, send them across two or three separate emails rather than cramming them all into one that itself exceeds the limit.

3. Send the parts, clearly labeled, and note the total count so nothing looks missing

For a legally binding document, mention that it was split only for transit, not altered.

A field report packed with site photos is 70MB.

1. Compress it first to optimize the images

Your company's email server rejects anything over 10MB, and you have a 28MB proposal.

1. Compress the proposal to cut it down
2. Split the result into three parts, each safely under 10MB

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Compress before splitting** — fewer, smaller parts and less hassle for the recipient
- **Split along logical boundaries** — chapters or sections are easier to follow than arbitrary page cuts
- **Number your files clearly** — `Part1_of_3` beats `document (1)`
- **Leave headroom under the limit** — encoding overhead means aim well below the cap
- **Tell the recipient the plan** — a one-line note prevents "did I get everything?" replies
- **Consider a shared link for very large files** — Gmail's Drive integration or a cloud link may beat sending many parts

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Splitting a document that should stay whole** — a single signed contract is cleaner compressed than chopped up
- **Sizing parts right at the limit** — encoding overhead pushes them over; aim lower
- **Vague file names** — the recipient can't reassemble parts they can't tell apart
- **Forgetting to compress first** — you may not need to split at all
- **Sending all parts in one email** — that combined email can itself exceed the limit

## When to Skip Email Entirely

If you find yourself splitting a file into five or six parts, email is fighting you. At that point, a shared cloud link is kinder to everyone. Upload the single PDF to a drive and send the link, so the recipient downloads one clean file instead of reassembling a pile of parts. Splitting is ideal for two or three parts; beyond that, a link usually wins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is splitting a PDF for email free?

Yes. The [Split PDF tool](/split-pdf) is free, runs in your browser, and needs no signup or software. Upload your large PDF, choose your page ranges, and download the separate parts at no cost. There's no watermark and no size trick that demands payment partway through.

### Will splitting reduce the quality of my PDF?

No. Splitting simply divides the pages into separate files; it doesn't recompress or degrade anything. Each part contains the exact same pages at the exact same quality as the original. Only compressing changes the file's internal quality, and even then it's usually not visible on screen.

### Should I split or compress my PDF for email?

Compress first, since it often gets the whole file under the limit in one step, which is simpler for your recipient. Split when the file is still too big after compressing, or when the recipient only needs certain sections. Many large files benefit from both: compress with the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf), then split the result if needed.

### How do I know how many parts I need?

Divide your file size by your email limit and round up, then add a part for safety because of encoding overhead. For a 45MB file on a 25MB limit, aim for three parts of roughly 15MB each rather than two of 22MB. Check each part's size before sending and split further if any part is too close to the cap.

### Can the recipient combine the parts back into one PDF?

Yes. They can reassemble the parts in order using the [Merge PDF tool](/merge-pdf), which stitches the files back into a single document. Just send the parts with clear numbers so they know the correct order to merge them in.
