You've finished a document, and now there's a small decision that quietly matters: do you save it as a PDF or send the DOCX? Pick the wrong one and your carefully formatted layout arrives scrambled on someone else's screen, or a document you meant to lock down turns up edited in ways you never intended.

Most people treat this as a coin flip. It isn't. PDF and DOCX are built for different jobs, and once you understand what each is good at, the choice is obvious almost every time.

This guide breaks down how the two formats differ, when to reach for each, and how to move between them freely when you need to. No format is "better" overall — they're tools for different moments.

## How PDF and DOCX Are Different at Their Core

PDF (Portable Document Format) was designed to look identical everywhere. When you open a PDF on a phone, a Windows laptop, a Mac, or a printer, it looks exactly the same. The layout is locked. The fonts are embedded. Nothing moves.

DOCX (the Word format) was designed to be edited. It stores your content as structured, reflowable text. The same DOCX can look slightly different on different devices depending on which fonts are installed, what version of Word is running, and how the page is sized. That flexibility is a feature when you're writing, and a liability when you're sharing.

| Feature | PDF | DOCX |
|---|---|---|
| Looks identical on every device | Yes | Not always |
| Editable text and formatting | No (without special tools) | Yes |
| Fonts embedded | Yes | Depends on settings |
| File size | Usually smaller for finished docs | Can be larger with tracked changes |
| Accepted by most systems | Yes | Usually, but not always |
| Supports tracked changes | No | Yes |
| Searchable text | Yes (if text-based) | Yes |
| Password protection | Yes | Yes |
| Fillable forms | Yes | Limited |
| Printing fidelity | Exact | May vary |

## When to Use PDF

**Sharing a finished document.** Once a document is done — a report, a proposal, an invoice, a contract — PDF is the right format. The recipient sees exactly what you designed, regardless of their device or software.

**Sending to someone who shouldn't edit it.** A PDF isn't a security guarantee, but it signals "this is final" and prevents accidental edits. For contracts, certificates, and official letters, PDF is the expected format.

**Submitting to a portal or form.** Most government portals, job application systems, and official submission platforms expect PDFs. They're universally readable and don't depend on the recipient having Word installed.

**Printing.** PDFs print exactly as they appear on screen. DOCX files can shift slightly depending on the printer driver and the version of Word on the printing machine.

**Archiving.** For long-term storage, PDF is more reliable. A DOCX file depends on software that can read it; a PDF is self-contained and will look the same in twenty years.

**Protecting layout-sensitive documents.** Brochures, resumes, certificates, and anything with precise visual design should be PDF. A DOCX version of a carefully designed resume can fall apart on a different machine.

## When to Use DOCX

**When the document is still being written or edited.** DOCX is a working format. It supports tracked changes, comments, version history, and real-time collaboration. If a document is going back and forth between people for edits, DOCX is the right tool.

**When you need to collaborate.** Google Docs, Microsoft Word, and most word processors work natively with DOCX. Sending a DOCX for review invites edits; sending a PDF signals that the document is done.

**When the content will be reformatted.** If someone needs to take your content and reformat it — change the layout, update the branding, translate it — DOCX is far easier to work with than a PDF.

**When you're working from a template.** Most document templates are DOCX. Filling in a contract template, a report template, or a letter template is a DOCX job. Convert to PDF when you're done.

**When the recipient needs to fill in fields.** Simple form fields in a Word document are easier to edit than a PDF form for most users, though PDF forms are more reliable across devices.

## The Practical Decision Tree

Most of the time, the choice is simple:

- Still writing or editing? → DOCX
- Sending for review or collaboration? → DOCX
- Finished and ready to share? → PDF
- Submitting to a portal or official system? → PDF
- Printing? → PDF
- Archiving? → PDF
- Recipient needs to edit the content? → DOCX
- Recipient just needs to read it? → PDF

The pattern: DOCX for working, PDF for sharing.

## Real-World Scenarios

### Sending a Finished Report

You've written a quarterly report in Word and it's ready to go out to stakeholders. Send it as PDF. The layout will look identical on every device, nobody can accidentally edit it, and it prints exactly as designed.

Keep the DOCX for your own records in case you need to update it next quarter.

### Collaborating on a Draft Contract

A contract is going back and forth between two parties for edits. Both sides need to add comments, track changes, and revise clauses. DOCX is the right format here — it's built for exactly this kind of back-and-forth.

Your resume is a carefully designed DOCX. Before submitting, convert it to PDF. This ensures the recruiter sees your layout exactly as you built it, regardless of which version of Word they have or what fonts are installed on their machine.

You want to store signed agreements, certificates, and official correspondence for the long term. PDF is the right choice. It's self-contained, doesn't depend on any particular software version, and will look the same when you open it in ten years.

## Tips for Choosing and Converting

- **Default to PDF for anything final** — if the document is done, PDF is almost always right
- **Keep the DOCX as your master copy** — convert to PDF for each version you send, but edit the DOCX
- **Convert scanned PDFs with OCR** before turning them into Word, using the [OCR PDF tool](/ocr-pdf)
- **Check formatting after every conversion** — PDF-to-Word especially can shift complex layouts
- **Don't convert back and forth repeatedly** — each round trip can nudge your formatting
- **Compress large PDFs** if you hit an upload limit, with the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf)
- **Embed fonts** when creating PDFs from unusual typefaces so they display correctly everywhere

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is PDF or DOCX better?

Neither is better overall — they're built for different jobs. DOCX is the format for writing and editing; PDF is the format for sharing, printing, and archiving finished documents. The right choice depends entirely on what you're doing with the document at that moment.

### Can I edit a PDF directly, or do I have to convert it?

You can edit PDFs with specialized tools, but for substantial text changes, converting to Word is usually easier. Use the [PDF to Word tool](/pdf-to-word) to turn the PDF into an editable DOCX, make your changes, then convert back to PDF with the [Word to PDF tool](/word-to-pdf) when you're done.

### Will converting between formats ruin my formatting?

Converting DOCX to PDF is reliable — the PDF looks like your Word document with fonts locked in. Converting PDF to DOCX is generally good for text-based files, though very complex layouts may need minor cleanup. Avoid converting back and forth many times, as each round trip can shift things slightly.

### Why does my DOCX look different on someone else's computer?

DOCX depends on the fonts and software installed on the reader's machine. If they don't have your font, Word substitutes another one, which shifts your layout. This is exactly why you convert to PDF before sharing — a PDF embeds the fonts and locks the layout so it looks identical everywhere.

### Which format should I use for a resume or CV?

Send it as a PDF. A resume is layout-sensitive, and a PDF guarantees the recruiter sees your design exactly as intended. Keep an editable DOCX for updates, but convert to PDF with the [Word to PDF tool](/word-to-pdf) before every submission.
