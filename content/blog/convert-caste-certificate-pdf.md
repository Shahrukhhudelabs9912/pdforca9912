The scholarship portal wants your caste certificate and income certificate uploaded as PDFs, each under 200KB. You have the certificates as photos on your phone — one is 5MB, the other is sideways, and the deadline is tomorrow. The portal keeps throwing errors, and re-clicking the photo doesn't help.

This is a rite of passage for students applying for scholarships, reserved-category seats, fee concessions, and government schemes across India. Caste certificates (SC/ST/OBC), income certificates, and EWS certificates are among the most-uploaded documents on portals like the National Scholarship Portal and state admission sites — and they all have strict format and size rules.

The fix takes two minutes with just your phone and a browser. No scanner, no cyber café, no paid app. Here's how to scan and convert your certificate into a clean PDF that portals will accept.

## What You'll Need

- Your caste certificate, income certificate, or EWS certificate — the original, a print, or a downloaded copy
- A phone or laptop with a browser
- Two minutes
- No signup, no app, no payment

## Why Portals Are So Strict About These Certificates

Caste and income certificates decide eligibility for scholarships, reserved seats, and fee waivers, so the portals that collect them are unusually strict. Understanding their rules saves you a lot of failed uploads.

| What portals want | Why it matters |
|-------------------|----------------|
| PDF format | Keeps every page together, looks official, harder to tamper with |
| Very small size (often 100-500KB) | Portals handle lakhs of uploads and cap size hard |
| Readable text and clear seal | Officials verify the certificate number and issuing authority |
| Correct orientation | A sideways certificate can be rejected on sight |

Many state portals and the National Scholarship Portal set limits as tight as 200KB for a single certificate. That's smaller than most phone photos, which is why simply uploading a gallery image almost always fails. The trick is converting to PDF and then compressing to fit.

## Step-by-Step: Scan and Convert Your Certificate

### Step 1: "Scan" With Your Phone Camera

You don't need a scanner. A careful phone photo works just as well if you get the basics right:

1. Lay the certificate flat on a plain, dark surface
2. Use daylight from a window — avoid tube-light glare on the paper and seal
3. Hold the phone directly above, parallel to the page, so edges stay straight
4. Fill the frame with the certificate, leaving a thin border
5. Confirm the certificate number, your name, and the official seal are all sharp

Skip heavy filters or "scanner" enhancements that can blur the fine print. A clean, honest photo verifies more easily than an over-processed one.

### Step 2: Convert the Photo to PDF

Open the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf) in your browser and upload the photo. If your certificate runs to a second page, upload both images together so they become one PDF.

Arrange the pages in order and fix orientation if the certificate came out sideways. Then generate and download the PDF.

### Step 3: Check the File Size

Open the downloaded PDF and confirm it looks correct — text sharp, seal visible, nothing cut off. Then check the file size. Government portals often cap uploads at 200KB, 500KB, or 1MB.

If your PDF is over the limit, run it through the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf). A single-page certificate photo compresses comfortably under 200KB without losing readability. Don't re-shoot at lower quality — compressing a good PDF gives a cleaner result than a blurry original.

### Step 4: Name the File Clearly

Rename the PDF before uploading. Something like `Priya_Sharma_Caste_Certificate.pdf` is far easier to track than `IMG_20261012_184522.pdf`, and it looks more professional when a portal or official reviews your submission.

### Step 5: Upload to the Portal

Log in to the portal, find the certificate upload field, and attach your PDF. Most portals show a green tick or a filename confirmation when the upload succeeds. If it fails, check the size limit and format requirement — both are usually listed near the upload button.

## Tips for the Best Scan Quality

The certificate number, your name, and the official seal are what officials verify. If any of them is unreadable, the certificate can be rejected even if it is genuine. Get these right:

- **Use daylight, not tube light** — position the certificate near a window. Tube lights create glare on the paper and wash out the embossed seal.
- **Lay the paper flat** — a curled or folded certificate throws shadows across the text. Flatten it under a book for a minute first.
- **Shoot straight down** — hold the phone parallel to the page so the edges stay square. An angled shot distorts the text and looks unprofessional.
- **Fill the frame** — get close enough that the certificate fills most of the photo, leaving only a thin border. This keeps the fine print sharp after compression.
- **Skip the beauty filters** — "scanner" enhancements and auto-filters can smear the fine print and alter the seal colour. A clean, honest photo verifies more easily.
- **Check the seal and signature** — before you convert, zoom in and confirm the round seal and the issuing officer's signature are both clearly visible.
- **Compress, don't re-shoot** — if the file is too big, compress a good PDF rather than taking a lower-quality photo. Compression keeps text sharper than a deliberately blurry original.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does my certificate photo get rejected even though it looks fine?

Portals reject uploads for three common reasons: the file is over the size limit, it is in the wrong format (JPG instead of PDF), or the seal and certificate number are not clearly readable. Convert to PDF with the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf), compress under the stated limit with the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf), and double-check that the seal is sharp before uploading.

### How do I get my caste certificate PDF under 200KB?

Convert the photo to a PDF first, then run it through the [Compress PDF tool](/compress-pdf). A single-page certificate compresses comfortably under 200KB while keeping the text and seal legible. Avoid re-shooting the photo at low quality — compressing a good scan gives a far cleaner result than a blurry original.

### Can I combine my caste and income certificate into one PDF?

Only if the portal explicitly asks for a combined document. Most portals want each certificate uploaded in its own labelled slot as a separate PDF. If a form does ask for one file, use the [Merge PDF tool](/merge-pdf) to combine them, then compress the merged file to fit the limit.

### Does the certificate need to be in colour or is black-and-white fine?

Colour is safer. Many issuing seals and signatures are in blue or red ink, and officials use them to verify authenticity. A colour scan or photo preserves these details. Black-and-white can lose the seal's detail and occasionally leads to rejection, so shoot in colour and compress rather than converting to grayscale.

### My certificate is two pages. How do I make it one PDF?

Photograph both pages, then upload both images together to the [JPG to PDF tool](/jpg-to-pdf) — they become a single two-page PDF in the order you arrange them. Confirm both pages are legible and correctly oriented, then compress the combined file if it exceeds the portal's size limit.
