You're filling out an online application — a passport renewal, a scholarship form, a job portal on a government website — and everything is going fine until the upload box rejects your document. "File size must be less than 1 MB." Your scanned PDF is 4.2 MB, and the portal won't budge.

This is a daily frustration on Indian government portals and application sites worldwide. Many were built with tight limits — 1 MB, sometimes 500 KB, occasionally 200 KB per file — and they enforce them strictly. A single high-resolution scan can blow past that in one page.

The fix isn't to delete pages or lower your standards. It's to compress the PDF properly, so it slips under the limit while staying perfectly readable. This guide shows you exactly how, plus how to hit even harsher limits when 1 MB isn't small enough.

## What You'll Need

- The PDF that's too large to upload

## Why Are PDFs So Big in the First Place?

Understanding what makes a PDF heavy tells you how to shrink it. Most oversized PDFs are big for one reason: **images**.

- **Scans and photos** are the usual culprit. A phone camera shoots at 12 megapixels or more. Drop that into a PDF and a single page can weigh several megabytes.
- **High DPI scans.** Scanning at 600 DPI when 150–200 DPI is plenty for a form triples or quadruples the size.
- **Embedded fonts and metadata.** These add weight, though far less than images.
- **Repeated or uncompressed image data.** Some scanners save every page as a full-resolution bitmap with no compression.

Compression targets exactly this. It downsamples oversized images to a sensible resolution, applies efficient image compression, and strips redundant data — shrinking the file dramatically while keeping text sharp and legible.

| What's in your PDF | Effect on size | What compression does |
|--------------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| Text and vector shapes | Tiny | Left mostly untouched |
| High-resolution photos | Huge | Downsampled and re-compressed |
| 600 DPI scans | Very large | Reduced to a readable DPI |
| Duplicate image data | Adds up | Removed |
| Metadata and unused objects | Small | Stripped out |

The key insight: text is cheap, images are expensive. That's why a text-only PDF is often already under 1 MB, while a two-page scan is 5 MB.

## When 1 MB Still Isn't Small Enough

Some portals demand 500 KB, 200 KB, or even a per-document photo limit. A few extra tactics get you there:

- **Compress, then check the number.** Often a single pass already clears these tighter limits.
- **Split the document.** If a portal wants each page under 200 KB separately, use the [Split PDF tool](/split-pdf) to break a multi-page PDF into single pages, then compress each one.
- **Extract only what's asked for.** If you only need to submit pages 3 and 4 of a long PDF, pull them out with the [Extract Pages tool](/extract-pages) first. A two-page file is far smaller than a twenty-page one.
- **Scan smarter next time.** If you're scanning fresh, choose 150–200 DPI and grayscale for text documents. Color and 600 DPI are overkill for most forms and multiply the size.

## Tips for the Best Result

- **Always check the exact limit first.** Knowing whether it's 1 MB, 500 KB, or 200 KB tells you how hard you need to push.
- **Compress the final version.** Do it after you've assembled the complete document, not before you add pages.
- **Scan at a sensible DPI.** 150–200 DPI grayscale is plenty for text forms and starts you off much smaller.
- **Keep the original.** Save your full-quality PDF separately in case you need it later at higher resolution.
- **Verify legibility every time.** For forms, ID, and financial documents, zoom in and confirm nothing important got blurry.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Deleting content to save size.** You rarely need to. Compression handles it without losing pages.
- **Over-compressing important scans.** If a signature or ID photo turns fuzzy, compress a little less or split and compress pages individually.
- **Ignoring the limit until the deadline.** Check and compress early so a rejected upload doesn't cost you the submission.
- **Uploading the wrong file.** After compressing, make sure you upload the smaller version, not the original.
